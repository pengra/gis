What is Gerrymandering?

Disclosure: I’m not a lawyer.

Precincts & Districts

Before we can define what gerrymandering is, we begin with a crash course in how voting in the United States works. Note that the United States is not a democracy; rather it is a Republican Democracy. Instead of citizens directly voting on issues, citizens vote on representatives that represent their interests in the federal and state government.
During the United States’ youth, it was decided that the states split their geography into “precincts” (synonymous to “voting district”). Precinct boundaries were drawn such that the voting population in each precinct was roughly the same. These precincts were then grouped into “districts,” where districts were subjected to similar requirements as precincts, but on a much larger scale. Each district had elected individuals that would represent the interests of the area in the federal and state government.
For instance, Washington state has around 5,500 precincts, and only 10 congressional districts – and 10 representatives in Congress.


Source: Wikipedia

Redistricting

As states shift in population sizes, states are granted more districts (or lose districts if the population shrinks). Additionally, states must balance the populations between districts between growing cities and/or growing rural areas. When this occurs, the state is tasked with redrawing district lines. This process is known as redistricting.
Redistricting policies vary from state to state. Some states have a non-partisan committee, while other states have the ruling party draw district lines (what could go wrong here?). Other states may have other policies.


Some Legal Redistricting Criteria

There are currently a few federal redistricting requirements.

  1. Representatives are to “be elected by districts composed of contiguous territory” (and only one representative per district)
  2. Districts are to have “as nearly as practicable an equal number of inhabitants”
  3. Racial and ethnic minorities in states must be able “to participate in the political process and to elect representatives of their choice.”

The last two requirements aren’t hard rules. There have been exceptions to both rules. Typically speaking, districts with even a 1% disparity are constitutionally suspicious, and may be rejected by the courts for being unconstitutional.
Additionally, if a minority population in a state is naturally diluted, and designing districts to force a minority majority in one district is impracticable, the requirement may be dropped.

Gerrymandering

As early as 1812, it became apparent that certain redistrictings, ensured elected officials could maintain their power – even if most voters didn’t vote for them. The Huffington Post infographic below demonstrates how this is possible.

As the infographic portrays, gerrymandering is the act of reducing competitiveness of districts to benefit a single party. Blatant gerrymandering is typically easy to spot, for instance, in the years 1993 - 1999, North Carolina’s 12th district was a textbook example of gerrymandering.


Source: Wikipedia

Unfortunately, gerrymandering is ultimately subjective. The district above could be argued to reach a specific demographic; such as fishermen, or certain ethnic groups. This is the inspiration for the Rakan project, to mathematically define gerrymandering.

Rakan's Assumptions & Definitions

For Rakan's purposes, for a redistricting to be "legal" it must fulfill the following conditions:

  • A district cannot be geographically separated into two parts; all precincts in a district must be directly bordering another precinct in the same district.
  • States with multi-polygonal precincts must be split into sub-precincts. Each sub-precinct should be considered as a single precinct while Rakan runs.
Redistrictings that are not meet these conditions are not considered a part of the solution set.

For a redistricting to be "good," it must fulfil the following conditions:

  • Districts are geographically compact, as in the number of precincts connected that are in different precincts are minimized.
  • Distrits represent the population fairly. For instance, if a state has $n$ districts, each state should have roughly $\frac{1}{n}$ th of the total population in each district.
The goal of Rakan isn't to find the optimal solution given these "good" parameters (It's already been "found"). Rather, Rakan hopes to be able to determine the probability of a redistricting being gerrymandered. This is achieved by comparing a redistricting in question to other possible redistrictings and measuring how it performs in a handful of metrics defined by legal requirements and by user defined metric requirements.
