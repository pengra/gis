What is Gerrymandering?

Before we can define what Gerrymandering is, we begin with a crash course in how voting in the United States works. The United States is not a democracy; rather it is a Republican Democracy. Instead of citizens directly voting on issues, citizens vote on representatives that represent their interests in the federal and state government.

During the United States’ youth, it was decided that the states split their geography into “precincts” (synonymous to “voting district”). Precinct boundaries were drawn such that the voting population in each precinct was roughly the same. These precincts were then grouped into “districts,” where districts were subjected to similar requirements as precincts, but on a much larger scale. Each district had elected individuals that would represent the interests of the area in the federal and state government.

As early as 1812, it became apparent that by simply changing the boundaries of districts, elected officials could maintain their power – even if most voters didn’t vote for them. The Huffington Post infographic below demonstrates how this is possible.

Unfortunately, gerrymandering is ultimately subjective. The district above could be argued to reach a specific demographic; such as fishermen, or certain ethnic groups. This is the inspiration for the Rakan project, to mathematically define gerrymandering.

Assumptions & Definitions

For Rakan's purposes, for a redistricting to be "legal" it must fulfill the following conditions:

  • A district cannot be geographically separated into two parts; all precincts in a district must be directly bordering another precinct in the same district.
  • States with multi-polygonal precincts must be split into sub-precincts. Each sub-precinct should be considered as a single precinct while Rakan runs.
Redistrictings that are not meet these conditions are not considered a part of the solution set.

For a redistricting to be "good," it must fulfil the following conditions:

  • Districts are geographically compact, as in the number of precincts connected that are in different precincts are minimized.
  • Distrits represent the population fairly. For instance, if a state has $n$ districts, each state should have roughly $\frac{1}{n}$ th of the total population in each district.
The goal of Rakan isn't to find the optimal solution given these "good" parameters (It's already been "found"). Rather, project Rakan's goal is to determine the probability that a given redistricting is gerrymandered.
